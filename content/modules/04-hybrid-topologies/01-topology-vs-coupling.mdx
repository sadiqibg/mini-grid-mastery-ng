These three words name three independent decisions. Mixing them up causes BoQ errors and
procurement mistakes.

| Decision | What it answers | Example choices |
|---|---|---|
| **Topology** | Which generation, storage, and conversion blocks exist? | Solar-only · solar-battery · solar-diesel · solar-battery-diesel · solar-wind-battery · solar-hydro |
| **Coupling architecture** | How those blocks join electrically | DC-coupled · AC-coupled · hybrid bus |
| **Control philosophy** | How the system decides what runs when | PV-first · diesel-priority · cycle-charging · load-following |

A solar-battery-diesel mini-grid (topology) can be DC-coupled (architecture) and run
load-following (control) — or AC-coupled and cycle-charging. Different choices, same topology.

## DC-coupled vs AC-coupled in one paragraph each

**DC-coupled**: PV → MPPT → battery DC bus → inverter → AC distribution. One conversion path
to the load. Efficient for off-grid because energy doesn't bounce in and out of inverters.
Typical for systems up to a few hundred kW; large strings of MPPTs feed a common DC bus.

**AC-coupled**: PV → string inverter → AC bus ← battery inverter ← battery. Two inverters,
both seeing the AC bus. Better for retrofits onto existing diesel networks, easier to add or
upgrade in phases, and the natural choice once you cross ~250 kW. Round-trip efficiency is
slightly lower because PV → battery passes through two inverters.

<Callout tone="info">
There is no universally "better" coupling. The right answer comes from system size, retrofit
context, expansion plans, and what local installers can actually maintain.
</Callout>

<NigerianExample>
A 60 kW peri-urban market mini-grid replacing a diesel genset typically lands on AC-coupled —
the genset stays on the AC bus during the transition, and the project can be commissioned in
stages without dropping service.
</NigerianExample>
