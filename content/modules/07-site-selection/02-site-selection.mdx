## Four criteria — and the order to apply them

| Criterion | Question it answers |
|---|---|
| **Community demand and willingness to pay** | Will this site generate enough revenue at a sustainable tariff? |
| **Solar resource adequacy** | Is the resource good enough, after derating, year-round? |
| **Grid proximity and interconnection potential** | Is the national grid likely to arrive in a way that strands the asset? |
| **Land availability and physical access** | Can you site, build, and maintain the system here? |

Apply them in roughly this order. A perfect solar resource is wasted on a site with no demand;
strong demand on inaccessible land is unworkable.

## Where the "good solar map" deceives

A satellite-derived irradiance map is a starting screen, not a verdict. Three things it can't see:

- **Local shading** — palms, baobabs, a 2-storey building west of the planned array. Shade
  analysis means a real site visit, not just a NASA POWER pull.
- **Soiling severity** — harmattan dust film, livestock dust, road grit. Some sites need monthly cleaning, others quarterly. The map doesn't know.
- **Microclimate** — sites near rivers and Atlantic coast have more morning fog than the
  regional average suggests; northern sites in the harmattan belt see more dust-haze days.

<NigerianExample>
A site shortlisted on satellite data alone showed 5.6 PSH annual average. On the ground:
acacia trees on the south fence threw 2 hours of afternoon shade onto a third of the planned
array footprint between November and March. Real PSH on the array, after shade losses, was
closer to 4.9. The system that would have made financial sense at 5.6 didn't at 4.9.
</NigerianExample>

## Three principal cost estimation methods

| Method | When to use | Accuracy |
|---|---|---|
| **Analogous** | Stage 1 screening — "this site looks like our last 250 kW deployment" | ±50% |
| **Parametric** | Stage 2 feasibility — cost per kWh installed, scaled by parameters | ±25% |
| **Bottom-up** | Stage 3 design — line-by-line BoQ from real quotes | ±10% |

Accuracy improves as the project information improves. Demanding bottom-up accuracy at Stage 1
slows everything down for no real gain; trusting analogous numbers at Stage 3 is reckless.
