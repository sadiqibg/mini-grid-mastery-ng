## Why a Gantt is not a schedule

A list of tasks with start and end dates is not a schedule until you've **linked dependencies**. A
schedule answers: *"If this task slips by a week, what is the new commissioning date?"* A list
of dates answers nothing.

## A representative mini-grid schedule

| # | Task | Typical duration | Depends on |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Site survey & WTP study | 2–3 weeks | — |
| 2 | Resource & shade assessment | 2 weeks | 1 |
| 3 | Preliminary design + LCoE | 2 weeks | 2 |
| 4 | Regulatory submission | 1 week (filing); **30 business days** clock | 3 |
| 5 | DisCo notification (interconnected only) | **15 business days** silence clock | 3 |
| 6 | Detailed design (COREN-signed) | 3 weeks | 3 |
| 7 | Procurement (long-lead: batteries, inverters) | 8–14 weeks | 6 |
| 8 | Civil works (control room, foundations, fencing) | 4 weeks | 6 |
| 9 | Installation | 3 weeks | 7, 8 |
| 10 | Commissioning (4-stage) | 1–2 weeks | 9 |
| 11 | NEMSA inspection & energisation | 1 week | 10 |
| 12 | Soft launch + handover | 2 weeks | 11 |

The **critical path** is the longest dependency chain. Slippage on a critical-path task pushes
commissioning by the same amount; slippage on a non-critical task can absorb into float.

In this typical schedule, **procurement** is the long pole — battery and inverter lead times
dominate. You can't shorten total project time by accelerating civils if procurement still
takes 12 weeks.

<Callout tone="warning">
Two tasks have **regulatory clocks**, not work durations: NERC's 30-business-day target on
applications and the DisCo's 15-business-day silence rule. Pad these — they are best-case.
</Callout>
