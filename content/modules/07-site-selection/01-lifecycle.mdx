Every project moves through these six stages. Each stage has a **gate question** — answer it
honestly before you spend the next stage's budget.

| # | Stage | Gate question |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Opportunity identification** | Is there a real, sustained demand here? |
| 2 | **Feasibility & assessment** | Does the engineering work, and can the unit economics close? |
| 3 | **Planning & design** | Is the regulatory pathway clear, and is the design buildable on this site? |
| 4 | **Procurement & mobilisation** | Are funds, components, and labour secured under signed contracts? |
| 5 | **Construction & commissioning** | Is the build passing each commissioning stage in order? |
| 6 | **Operations & handover** | Is the site meeting reliability, revenue, and safety targets month-on-month? |

## Where projects die

- **Stage 1 → 2.** Confusing initial enthusiasm with sustained demand. Validate with at least one season of evidence — diesel-genset records, market-day counts, willingness-to-pay surveys conducted in person.
- **Stage 2 → 3.** Skipping the financial close test. If LCoE-vs-WTP doesn't pencil, no amount of engineering rescues the project.
- **Stage 3 → 4.** Designing without knowing the regulator. NERC vs SERC reshapes timelines and documentation. Sort it before procurement.
- **Stage 5 → 6.** Treating commissioning as a formality. The faults caught at this stage are the only ones you'll get to fix cheaply.

<Callout tone="info">
Each gate exists to fail projects that shouldn't proceed. A killed project at Stage 2 is a win — it
saves the capital that would have been wasted at Stages 3–5.
</Callout>
