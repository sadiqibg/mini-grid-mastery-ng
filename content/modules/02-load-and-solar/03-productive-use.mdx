## Why domestic load alone won't bank a project

A community of 80 households consuming 0.4 kWh/day each produces ~32 kWh/day — not enough
revenue at any defensible tariff to recover capital and run the site. **Productive-use
customers** are what make the unit economics work:

- Grain or pepper mill (3–15 kW, midday)
- Welder or carpenter (3–8 kW, intermittent)
- Cold storage / cold room (1–5 kW, continuous + compressor spikes)
- Irrigation pump (1–7 kW, seasonal, dry-season peak)
- Phone-charging kiosk (0.3–1 kW, all day)
- Barber, tailor, hairdresser (0.3–2 kW, evening)
- Cassava or rice processing (5–20 kW, harvest season)

Productive use does three things at once:

1. Adds high revenue per customer.
2. Fills the **midday PV window**, raising self-consumption.
3. Anchors the community's willingness to pay because the loads earn money.

<NigerianExample>
A small cassava-processing cluster of three operators consumed ~40 kWh/day during harvest
season — more than 60 domestic customers combined. They also flattened the load profile,
which dropped required battery capacity ~30 % vs. a domestic-only design.
</NigerianExample>

## Surveying productive customers honestly

Three traps to avoid:

- **Hypothetical equipment.** "If we had reliable power we'd buy a welder" — don't size for it until they have a real plan to buy.
- **Aspirational hours.** Survey-stated 12 hours/day often means 3–5 hours of real machine time.
- **Single-phase vs three-phase.** A 5 kW three-phase mill needs different inverter and
  conductor planning than three 5 kW single-phase loads.

<Callout tone="warning">
A mini-grid sized around one big productive customer that hasn't yet bought their equipment is
the most common cause of project under-collection. Tie productive-use sizing to evidence —
existing diesel-genset records, market visits, signed letters of intent.
</Callout>
