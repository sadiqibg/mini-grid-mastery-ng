## Two numbers, not one

Every load profile carries two numbers that drive different parts of the system:

- **Peak power (kW)** — the highest instantaneous demand. Sets inverter and conductor sizing.
- **Daily energy (kWh)** — the area under the curve. Sets PV array and battery sizing.

A community that uses 100 kWh/day can have a 10 kW peak or a 40 kW peak. The PV array
needed is similar; the inverter, distribution conductors, and protection equipment are not.

<Callout tone="info">
A flat profile is cheaper to serve than a spiky one. If you can shift a grain mill from 6 pm
(coincident with lighting peak) to 11 am (coincident with PV peak), you reduce required
storage and may avoid an inverter upgrade.
</Callout>

## The shape of a Nigerian community profile

Three time bands typically dominate:

1. **Morning rise (5 am–9 am)** — irons, hot plates, water pumps, early commerce.
2. **Midday productive use (10 am–4 pm)** — mills, welders, cold rooms, fans.
3. **Evening domestic peak (6 pm–10 pm)** — lights, TVs, phones, fans. This is the biggest peak
   in most rural communities because it stacks on top of any commercial activity that's still
   open.

<NigerianExample>
In a northern agrarian community surveyed for a 60-customer mini-grid, midday demand was
~12 kW (irrigation + 2 mills), and evening peak was ~18 kW (mostly lights and phone charging
across 60 households). Same community, two different design constraints.
</NigerianExample>

## Why "estimated load × hours" misleads

Adding up nameplate ratings overestimates real demand:

- Not every appliance is on at once → apply a **coincidence factor** per appliance class.
- Seasonal use varies → apply a **seasonal factor** for fans, irrigation, cold storage.
- Productive-use customers under-report hours in a survey; verify with a meter reading.

This is why the Load Survey Lab on this site asks for *both* coincidence and seasonal factors.
Skipping them is the most common reason early projects oversize PV and undersize batteries.
