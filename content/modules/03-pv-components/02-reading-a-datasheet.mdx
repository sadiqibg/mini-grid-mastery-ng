## The six numbers you can't ignore

Every module datasheet quotes performance at **Standard Test Conditions** (STC): 1000 W/m²,
25 °C cell temperature, AM1.5 spectrum. Nigeria does not run at STC.

| Symbol | Meaning | What it constrains |
|---|---|---|
| **Pmax** | Maximum power at STC (W) | Headline number; informs cost-per-watt |
| **Vmp** | Voltage at maximum power | String sizing vs. controller MPPT window |
| **Imp** | Current at maximum power | Conductor and fuse sizing |
| **Voc** | Open-circuit voltage (no load) | Maximum string voltage — must not exceed controller max input |
| **Isc** | Short-circuit current | Combiner box, fuse, and conductor max |
| **Temp coefficient (Pmax)** | %/°C loss as cell warms | Real-world derating in Nigerian heat |

<Callout tone="warning">
**Voc rises in cold weather and falls in heat.** Counter-intuitively, the design check for max
string voltage uses the coldest expected morning temperature, not the hottest noon. Get this
wrong and you can blow controller inputs on a cool harmattan morning.
</Callout>

## Mono-Si is the practical default

Three reasons mono-Si dominates Nigerian mini-grid procurement:

1. Highest efficiency → less land or roof area for the same Wp.
2. Better high-temperature behaviour than polycrystalline.
3. Logistics: 350–550 Wp class modules are widely available locally and through Apapa imports.

Look for **IEC 61215** (performance) and **IEC 61730** (safety) certifications on the
datasheet. Both should be present for a module that will be deployed in a regulated mini-grid.

<NigerianExample>
A common 540 Wp mono module shows: Pmax 540 W, Vmp 41.6 V, Imp 12.98 A, Voc 49.5 V, Isc 13.85 A, temp coeff. –0.34 %/°C. With 8 modules in series → 332.8 V Vmp / 396 V Voc — fine for a 500 V controller, marginal for a 400 V one.
</NigerianExample>
