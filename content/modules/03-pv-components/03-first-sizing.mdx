## The two formulas you'll use first

For a quick sizing pass before you open a simulation tool:

```
PV array (W)        = Daily demand (Wh) / (PSH × derating)
Battery bank (Wh)   = Daily demand (Wh) × Autonomy days / DoD
```

- **Derating** lumps temperature, soiling, wiring, and conversion losses. Use **0.75** for early
  passes; refine when you have component data.
- **Autonomy days** = how many no-sun days the battery must carry. **1.0–1.5 days** is common
  for hybrid systems with a generator backup; **2–3 days** if there's no generator.
- **DoD (depth of discharge)** = the share of nameplate capacity you'll actually use per cycle.
  Lead-acid: **0.5**. Lithium iron phosphate (LFP): **0.8**.

## A worked example

Say a community needs **80 kWh/day**. Site PSH (rainy-season conservative) = **4.5**.

- **PV array** = 80,000 / (4.5 × 0.75) ≈ **23.7 kWp**. Round up to next clean string count — call it **24 kWp** with 540 Wp modules → ~45 modules.
- **Battery (LFP, 1.5 days autonomy)** = 80,000 × 1.5 / 0.8 = **150 kWh** nameplate.

Sanity check: a 48 V system carrying 150 kWh ≈ 3,125 Ah, which is unworkable at 48 V; this
project lives at **400–600 V battery bus** or in a high-voltage hybrid inverter architecture.
Voltage choice falls out of the size, not the other way around.

<Callout tone="warning">
The simple formula treats the battery as a perfect bucket. Real batteries have charge
efficiency, calendar ageing, and minimum SoC for warranty. Treat the 0.8 DoD and the autonomy
days as conservative buffers, not stretch goals.
</Callout>

## Why this pass isn't the final design

Everything above assumes a single energy total. It says nothing about:

- The **shape** of the load profile (peak power, midday vs evening).
- Generator dispatch (covered in Module 5).
- LCoE and tariff feasibility (covered in Module 6).
- Real-world contingency (covered in Module 7).

Use this pass for go/no-go scoping. Always come back with a full hybrid sizing — that's the
optimisation problem, not this.
