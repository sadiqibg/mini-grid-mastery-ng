## Two different numbers

- **Cost-reflective tariff** = the per-kWh price that recovers CAPEX, OPEX, fuel, replacements, and a return, given the energy actually served. It is calculated.
- **Willingness to pay (WTP)** = the per-kWh price customers actually accept and consistently pay. It is observed.

When they're equal, the project is bankable on tariff revenue alone. When they're not — which is most of the time in early-stage rural Nigerian markets — the gap has to be closed somehow.

| Cost-reflective ≈ WTP | Project survives on tariff alone |
| Cost-reflective > WTP | Subsidy, grant, or DFI debt closes the gap |
| WTP > Cost-reflective | Rare, but possible for high-productive-use clusters |

## How LCoE feeds the tariff

**LCoE (Levelised Cost of Electricity)** = NPC ÷ lifetime kWh served. It is a per-kWh number,
discounted to today, that includes every cost the project incurs over its life.

- LCoE is roughly the minimum sustainable tariff before profit and collection losses.
- Adjust LCoE up for **collection efficiency** (e.g. ÷ 0.85 if you expect 85 % collection).
- Adjust again for desired equity return.

Two projects with identical kWh/year can have very different LCoEs because of CAPEX,
fuel use, battery replacement intervals, and discount rate.

## When WTP < cost-reflective: four real options

1. **DARES PBG or MST grant.** Reduces effective CAPEX → lowers LCoE → tariff can be smaller.
2. **Anchor customer**. A productive-use anchor pays a higher tariff that cross-subsidises domestic customers.
3. **Tariff bands**. Different bands by customer class. Common, regulator-friendly.
4. **Scale down**. If no subsidy is available and WTP genuinely won't move, the project may need to wait or shrink.
