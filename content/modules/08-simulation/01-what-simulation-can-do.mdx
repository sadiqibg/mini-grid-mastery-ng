## Two tools, two questions

| Tool | Strongest at |
|---|---|
| **HOMER Pro** | Hybrid system optimisation — what combination of PV, battery, genset, and dispatch minimises LCoE under your constraints |
| **PVSyst** | Detailed PV system yield — what the array will produce hour-by-hour given site, tilt, shading, and degradation |

Most mini-grid developers use HOMER to settle the **sizing and dispatch question**, then bring
the chosen design into PVSyst (or directly into procurement) for **yield validation**.

## What simulation does well

- Compares hundreds of `(PV, battery, genset)` candidates against the same load profile and
  resource data — work that's not feasible by hand.
- Reports NPC, LCoE, unmet load, renewable fraction, fuel use per candidate.
- Stress-tests against fuel price scenarios.
- Quantifies the cost of an arbitrary reliability target ("99 % vs 99.9 %").

## What simulation does not do

- It does not **observe demand**. If your load profile is wrong, every output is wrong with
  conviction.
- It does not **value community trust**. A simulation may prefer a smaller battery; a community
  used to candle-light failures may need the bigger one to actually pay.
- It does not **schedule construction** or **clear regulators**.
- It does not catch **shading** that wasn't entered. Garbage in, polished garbage out.

<Callout tone="warning">
A simulation result that says LCoE = ₦187/kWh ± nothing is reporting precision, not accuracy.
Always sensitivity-test the inputs you're least sure about: load profile, fuel price, battery
replacement interval, discount rate.
</Callout>

<NigerianExample>
A common failure mode: a developer simulates with a "best estimate" load profile of 250 kWh/day
and gets a 60 kWp PV / 200 kWh battery design. Real metered load after six months is 180
kWh/day — the PV is curtailed daily, battery is under-cycled, and LCoE is 20 % worse than
modelled. The simulation wasn't wrong; the input was.
</NigerianExample>
