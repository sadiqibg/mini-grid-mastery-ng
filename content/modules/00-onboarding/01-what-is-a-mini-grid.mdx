## Why this matters in Nigeria

Tens of millions of Nigerians live in communities that the national grid either does not reach
or reaches only erratically. A **mini-grid** is one of the few options that can deliver
grid-quality electricity to a whole community without waiting for the central grid to arrive.

## The plain-English definition

A mini-grid is a small, **self-contained electricity network** that:

- generates power locally (usually solar PV, often combined with a battery and sometimes a diesel generator),
- distributes it through poles, wires, and meters to many customers in a defined area,
- and can run **isolated** from the national grid, **interconnected** with it, or both.

It is bigger than a single solar home system and smaller than a utility distribution feeder.

<NigerianExample>
A market cluster of 80 shops, a primary school, and a health centre in a community two hours
from the nearest 33 kV line might be a perfect mini-grid candidate. They share a single
generator, share the cost, and share the same kind of metered service a city customer gets.
</NigerianExample>

## How it differs from neighbours

| Option | Who it serves | Reliability | Typical cost per kWh |
|---|---|---|---|
| National grid | Everyone connected | Variable | Cheapest when it's on |
| Diesel genset | One business or compound | High when fuelled | Very expensive (fuel + O&M) |
| Solar home system | One household | Good for lighting/phones | Mid |
| **Mini-grid** | Whole community | High by design | Mid (cheaper as load grows) |

<Callout tone="info">
A mini-grid is not just "a bigger solar home system". The design problem is fundamentally
different: you have to plan for **dozens of customers with different load patterns**, manage
demand across the day, and operate a small utility business — billing, O&M, and customer service.
</Callout>
